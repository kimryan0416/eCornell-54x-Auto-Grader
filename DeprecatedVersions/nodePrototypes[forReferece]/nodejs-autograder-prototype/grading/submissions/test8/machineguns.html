Machine Guns

Historical Use of Machine Guns

The Maxim Machine gun, invented by Hiram Maxin in 1884, was the first practical self-powered machine gun in the world. It was quickly adopted by all the major European powers. In WW1, machine guns quickly proved their worth by being able to surpress huge numbers of soldiers relatively easily. However, these machine guns were very heavy and were mostly used in a defensive role. Countries began experimenting with light machine guns that could be brought along during an attack to support soldiers advancing on trenches.

In-Game

Used by the Support Class, machine guns have large magazines to provide supression and kill large groups of enemies. Supression is dealt by landing multiple shots near enemies, which blurs their vision, stops them from regenerating health, and increases the sway on their weapons making them more inaccurate.

Recommendations

For aggressive Support players who like to run and gun, the BAR's high fire rate and good hipfire accuracy make it unmatched for that role. The MG15 n.A is better for Supports who like to hunker down and provide suppression due to its massive magazine size. The Madsen MG offers a balance with good damage, a high fire rate, and average capacity.

Lewis Gun

The Lewis Gun is a machine gun that is most effective at medium to long range. It is the default gun of the support class, and it is a jack of a trades type of gun. It has a large magazine size and relatively low recoil, making it good at surpressing many enemies at long range. It comes in three different variants:

  • Low Weight: This variant will recover from recoil faster and have less recoil overall.
  • Supressive: This variant has a larger magazine and an optical sight for increased supression.
  • Optical: This variant has increased accuracy and an optical sight for long range engagements.

Invented by American Colonel Issac Newton Lewis in 1911, it was adopted by the British Army in 1914 after Lewis failed to convince the US Army to adopt it. It was first used for vehicles and aircraft, but was then used by infantry in 1916 after the older Vickers machine gun proved too heavy. It soon outnumbered the Vickers on the Western Front by almost 3:1, mostly because of its portability. It would also be briefly used by the US Army, though it much smaller numbers. After WWI, it was replaced by the Bren gun, though a few Lewis guns were used because of shortages.

M1909 Benet-Mercie

The M1909 is machine gun that is best used at medium to long range. Its damage does not drop off as fast as other machine guns, meaning it will retain a four shot kill at a longer range than any other machine gun. However like all light machine guns continous fire is needed to make the gun accurate. This causes issues with the weapons small capacity of 30 rounds, but reloading is very quick balancing this out. This weapon has three variants:

  • Storm: This variant has less recoil, a good all around choice.
  • Telescopic: This variant has a tripod and a scope, better for very long ranges.
  • Optical: This variant has a reflex sight and a bipod, good for long range.

The M1909 Benet-Mercie was originally built by the French and known as the Mle 1909. It was purchased by the US and is considered to be the US Army's first light machine gun. It was first used to fight against Mexican rebel and government forces in the Border War of 1910-1919, where it proved to be a capable weapon. The M1909 was purchased by countries on both sides of WWI, but the limited production meant that they were a rare sight.

Madsen Machine Gun

The Madsen is primarily a close range machine gun. It has a good rate of fire, hipfire accuracy, and reload speed, which allow it to easily chew throught enemies without the need to aim down sights. However, if you try to aim down sights the sight itself is very obtrusive, limiting the Madsen's long range performance. The Madsen comes in three variants:

  • Low Weight: This variant will recover from recoil faster and has a bipod, making it good for longer ranges.
  • Trench: This variant will have better hipfire accuracy, better for close range.
  • Storm: This variant will have lower recoil, a good choice for any range.

The Madsen machine gun is a Danish machine gun designed in 1896. It was an expensive because of it sophisicated design, but its reliability made it worth its price. It was first used in the Russo-Japanese War of 1904-1905 by the Imperial Russian Army, and was purchased by many more countries after that. Before and after WWI, 34 countries would purchase the Madsen, and it would see service well into the 50's.

MG 15 n.A

The MG 15 n.A's main strength is its huge capacity. Starting with 100 rounds in a magazine, it can surpress huge groups of enemies for a long time. It also has the fastest muzzle velocity of any machine gun, requiring less of a lead for distant targets. However, these strenghs are balanced by low damage, requiring a lot of bullets to land to drop someone. The gun also overheats after firing 30 round continuously, so make sure to use bursts. The MG 15 n.A has 3 variants:

  • Low Weight: This variant will recover from recoil faster and has a bipod, making it good for longer ranges.
  • Storm: This variant has lower recoil and no bipod, better for aggresive play.
  • Surpressive: This variant increases the capacity to 200 and adds a bipod and reflex sight, allowing you to deploy and lay torrents of withering fire.

The MG15 n.A was the German answer to the British Lewis gun after the infamous Battle of the Somme. Realizing they had no counter to the Lewis Gun, the Germans took the MG15, a heavy water cooled gun and lightened it to make the MG15 n.A. Unfortunately, the MG15 n.A would be overshadowed by the MG 08/15, the light machine gun version of the MG 08 heavy machin gun. The MG15 n.A would mostly be used by German soldiers assisting the Ottoman Empire, and would largely be forgotten by WWII.

BAR 1918

The BAR 1918 is the machine gun most suited for close range. It has an extremely fast rate of fire and good damage, allowing it to tear through enemies. This combined with good hipfire accuracy makes the BAR a monster in close quarters. However, the BAR's main weakness is its capacity of only 20 rounds, meaning that taking down more than two enemies will usually require a reload. However, reloads are quite quick. The BAR has three variants:

  • Trench: This variant improves hipfire accuracy, better for close range fights.
  • Storm: This variant decreases recoil, better for medium range.
  • Telescopic: This variant adds a bipod and a scope for picking off enemies at long range.

Standing for Browning Automatic Rifle, the BAR was America's answer to a need for a light machine gun. Meant to support an infantry attack, the BAR was meant to implement a concept known as walking fire, where the weapon would be fired from the hip while hanging from a specialized shoulder strap. In reality, it would be used like a traditional light machine gun, fired from a bipod mounted on the ground. Although it would arrive very late in WWI, it would be the US's standard squad machine gun in WWII for the entire war and would see service even up to Vietnam.

Huot Automatic

Similar to the Lewis Gun, the Huot Automatic is a jack of all trades light machine gun. However, the Huot is more accurate than the Lewis, but also has less capacity and a lower rate of fire. To maximize your performance stay at long range and take your shots carefully. The Huot has two variants:

  • Low Weight: This variant recovers from recoil faster.
  • Optical: This variant adds a reflex sight and has slightly better accuracy to make long range shooting easier.

An automatic conversion of the Canadian Ross rifle after it was withdrawn from frontline service, the Huot Automatic Rifle was intended as Canada's light machine gun. Copying the cooling system of the Lewis Gun and adding a 25 round magazine, the Huot was popular with troops testing it in France, and was slated for full production. Unfortunately, the war ended before any guns could reach Europe.

Chauchat

The Chauchat is a unique light machine gun, mostly because of its incredible damage. It is capable of a three shot kill, the only machine gun capable of doing so. However, this is balanced by its extremely slow rate of fire of only 360 rounds per minute. To use this gun effectively you'll want to stay at a short to medium range to take advantage of the high damage, since at both very close and long range rate of fire is the most important factor in victory and the Chauchat's sluggish rate of fire will leave you outgunned. The Chauchat comes in two variants:

  • Low Weight: This variant recovers from recoil faster.
  • Telescopic: This variant adds a scope to assist with long range firefights.

The Chauchat, developed as far back as 1903, was adopted in 1915 as France's light machine gun. France had realized that its troops needed a weapon that could provide automatic fire and was light enough to be carried by one man in an attack. Unfortunately, it quickly gained an infamous reputation for unreliability in the muddy conditions of trench warfare. These failures usually stemmed from the Chauchat's unique half-moon magazine. It was open on one side to allow soldiers to see how many rounds they had left, but this also allowed dirt to enter and clog up the gun. The Americans especially hated the gun, as the Chauchats converted to American calibers often jammed after a few rounds due to differences in the amount of powder between French and American cartridges. However, the weapon was light compared to other light machine guns, and was inexpensive to produce, allowing the French to equip two armies with them. In the end, the Chauchat was a flawed weapon, but it was important in helping develop the infantry assault tactics that are used today.
